# Developer Marketing Manifesto

## Core Thesis

Developers adopt workflows, not features.

Developer marketing should function as developer enablement. Its job is to make useful workflows visible, reduce friction to first success, and help developers understand how a tool fits into the way they already work.

## Mental Model

A strong developer story follows the work:

- **Operate** — understand systems, explore problems, inspect state
- **Craft** — write, refactor, debug, and iterate
- **Ship** — review, govern, merge, and deploy

## Strategic Insight

Most tools optimize for one step. Strong platforms connect the full workflow.

The marketing implication:

> If workflows are the unit of value, they must be the unit of storytelling.

## What Marketing Should Do

- Tell workflow stories, not feature stories.
- Show real tasks, not abstract capabilities.
- Emphasize how one step connects to the next.
- Focus on time-to-first-success.
- Reduce friction to a developer's first successful workflow completion.

## What to Avoid

- Isolated feature positioning.
- Model comparisons as the primary narrative.
- Treating terminal, IDE, web, docs, or collaboration surfaces as disconnected.
- Claims that cannot be backed by lived product experience.

## Growth Model

Workflow completion creates habit.

Habit drives retention.

Retention creates expansion.
